The Christmas Sing

December 24, 2024 StoryCast Editorial Team Winter Traditions

Every Christmas Eve, the people of the village gathered in the old town hall for a celebration known simply as The Christmas Sing.

Families arrived carrying lanterns and wrapped in heavy winter coats. The hall glowed with warm candlelight while evergreen decorations filled the room with the scent of pine.

As the clock struck eight, the choir began the first song. Voices rose together, filling the hall with music that echoed through the snowy streets outside.

For many villagers, the Christmas Sing was more than a performance. It was a tradition that connected generations and reminded everyone of the importance of community, kindness, and celebration.

00:00Narrator: The following is The Christmas Sing in Our Village by Mary E. Wilkins Freeman, narrated by Beth Richmond.

00:18Narrator: The singing school is a regular institution in our village during the winter months, but the one of special interest is held on Christmas Eve — called the Christmas Sing.

00:35Narrator: On that night only the psalms and fugues appropriate to the occasion are sung, and the town hall is trimmed with holly and evergreen. The sing begins at eight o'clock and is always preceded by a turkey supper.

00:52Narrator: The supper is at the tavern — now we say hotel — the same old house where the stages drew up before the railroad was built. At least two hours are required to dispose of the good things and speechify.

01:10Narrator: Then the people cross the road to the town hall where the sing is held. It is a great occasion and the women give as much care to their costumes as if they were going to a ball.

01:28Narrator: The dressmaker is hard worked for weeks before the sing. Everybody who can afford it has a new dress, and those who cannot have their old ones made over.

02:05Narrator: The sing of last year was noteworthy in another respect — there were three betrothals and a runaway marriage that night. It was ideal weather: very cold and clear, a full moon, and beautiful snow for sleighing.

02:45Narrator: At six o'clock everybody was assembled at the tavern. Past and present members of the singing school, even old man Veazey who was over ninety, were there.

03:05Narrator: One of the best speeches was made by Mr. Cassius C. Dowell from East Langham — a fine tenor singer and quite a celebrity who sings in the church choir and is in great demand at funerals.

03:25Narrator: Lottie Green sat next to him at supper. Mr. Lucius Downey was on her other side, but she paid no attention to him.

03:48Narrator: Lorinda Snell looked very well in a new silk dress, plum color with velvet trimming. Her cheeks were pink, her eyes bright, and her hair most beautifully crimped.

04:10Narrator: Lottie Green had made over her old blue cloth dress and added silk sleeves — it looked as good as new.

04:32Narrator: Probably Maria Rice had the finest new dress — pink silk with a square, low-cut waist. A most startling innovation in our village.

04:55Narrator: Maria wore a great bunch of pink roses on one shoulder. The other girls were half admiring, half scandalized. No such costume had ever been worn to the singing school before.

05:15Narrator: Poor Zephyretta Stockwell, in a black silk that might have suited her grandmother, was entirely eclipsed by Maria — in more senses than one.

05:38Narrator: When supper was over the people assembled in the town hall. Green wreaths hung in all the windows, and the portrait of Squire Ebenezer Adams was draped with an American flag.

06:00Narrator: Our singing master Mr. Orlando Sage stood on the stage and conducted. At eight o'clock he raised his baton, and the music began.

06:22Narrator: Everybody stood up and sang their best and loudest. The old fugues were grand — had it not been for the weakness in the sopranos.

06:45Narrator: Mr. Sage waved his baton frantically at the sopranos. Finally, with a stamp of his foot, he brought the whole school to a dead stop.

07:08Mr. Sage: "Miss Stockwell — why don't you sing?"

07:15Narrator: Everybody stared at Zephyretta. She turned white, then red, and replied meekly that she was singing.

07:30Mr. Sage: "No you are not. I rode past your father's yesterday and heard you — you have a voice. Why don't you sing?"

07:52Narrator: Zephyretta looked as if she would cry. Then Lorinda Snell suddenly spoke for her.

08:15Lorinda Snell: "I can tell you why, Mr. Sage. Three years ago I heard Maria Rice tell Zephyretta not to sing so loud — she drowned her out — and Zephyretta hasn't sung loud since."

08:40Narrator: When Lorinda stopped, with a defiant nod, you could have heard a pin drop. Maria Rice was blushing as pink as her dress.

09:00Narrator: Mr. Sage brought his baton down. "Sing!" he shouted, and we all began again with "When Shepherds Watch Their Flocks by Night."

09:22Narrator: Zephyretta let out her voice — and we were all amazed. Nobody had dreamed she could sing so well. Maria did not sing again that evening.

09:45Narrator: At ten o'clock we closed. Many a young woman who had come with her father went home in the sleigh of a gallant swain waiting outside.

10:05Narrator: Maria Rice was seen lingering and peering toward Jim Payne, then gathering up her pink skirts to walk forlornly home with Lydia Wheelock.

10:28Narrator: She was barely on the road before Jim Payne and Zephyretta passed her in his sleigh, drawn by his fast black horse. Everybody was astonished.

10:50Narrator: Mr. Dowell took Lottie Green home. Mr. Downey escorted Lorinda Snell with a lantern — and in its light she looked like a young girl.

11:35Narrator: The next day we heard all three couples were to be married — and that another young couple had already driven ten miles to Dover to wed that very Christmas Eve.

11:58Narrator: Jim and Zephyretta are a devoted pair. Lorinda makes a good wife for Lucius Downey. Cassius Dowell seems very happy with Lottie.

12:22Narrator: Take it all together — that sing seemed to bring much happiness to our village, set, as it were, to sweet Christmas music.

12:48Narrator: Thank you for listening.
